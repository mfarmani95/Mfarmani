Soil Moisture Memory (SMM) and Its Role in Land Surface Modeling

Soil Moisture Memory (SMM) refers to the duration over which soil retains the effects of a moisture perturbation, such as rainfall, before returning to its equilibrium state. This concept is essential for understanding land–atmosphere interactions, as it encapsulates the temporal variations of soil moisture and reflects the exchange of fluxes between land and the atmosphere.

SMM serves as a crucial metric for evaluating Land Surface Models (LSMs), which are designed to simulate surface flux exchanges and provide boundary conditions for atmospheric models. By facilitating comparisons between observed and modeled soil moisture dynamics, SMM offers insights into the mechanisms within LSMs and their hydrometeorological responses.

Importance of Accurate Land Models

The efficacy of LSMs in simulating climate feedback mechanisms depends critically on accurately representing soil water retention capacity and soil moisture persistence.

Key Findings from Our Study

1. Influence of Soil Hydraulic Parameterization – The Brooks-Corey model enhances surface moisture but overestimates long-term SMM, while van Genuchten improves root-zone dynamics.

2. Role of Soil Macropores – Macropores enable rapid infiltration, altering SMM by affecting vertical redistribution and drainage.

3. Impact of Surface Water Ponding – Increasing ponding thresholds enhances infiltration and extends long-term SMM.

Implications for Climate and Water Management

Improved representation of soil processes strengthens climate prediction, drought management, and water resource planning.

Conclusion & Future Directions

This study shows how improved hydrological parameterizations in LSMs reduce biases in soil moisture memory and lead to better land–atmosphere coupling.
